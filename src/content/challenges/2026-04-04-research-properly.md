There is probably something you have been hearing about lately that you have not had time to actually look into. You nod when it comes up. You kind of know what it is. But if someone asked you to explain it, you would struggle.

This weekend, you are going to research that thing. Not with Google. With AI. And not by asking one question and accepting the first answer.

Most people use AI the same way they use a search engine: type a question, read the result, close the tab. That works for quick lookups. It is not research.

This challenge uses a technique called **chained prompting**. Instead of asking one question and stopping, you ask, read what AI gives you, then ask again based on what you just learned. Each prompt builds on the one before it. By the end, you have had a real research session, not a single lookup.

Here is how to do it.

---

## Step 1: Pick your topic

Think of one thing that has been coming up in your work or your life lately that you do not fully understand. It could be a term you keep hearing in meetings. A trend in your industry. A tool your company is starting to use. A news story you have been skimming past.

It does not need to be complicated. It just needs to be something you genuinely want to understand better.

Open Gemini at gemini.google.com. Web search is already on.

If you are using a different AI tool like Copilot, ChatGPT, or Claude, check if web search is enabled. The steps below work the same on any AI with web search on. If web search is not available, it may not be enabled for your account or license.

---

## Step 2: Ask your first question

Start simple. Just ask AI to explain it.

> "Can you explain {the topic you picked}? Give me a quick overview first, then I will ask follow-up questions."

Read what it gives you. Do not stop here. This is just the first link in the chain.

---

## Step 3: Go deeper, and try ELI10

Pick one part of the answer that did not fully click. Ask AI to go deeper on that part.

> "You mentioned {specific part of the answer}. Can you explain that in more detail?"

If the explanation is still too dense, add this line to any prompt:

> "Explain it like I am 10 years old."

This is called **ELI10**, short for "Explain Like I'm 10." It is not just for beginners. People use it to cut through jargon and get to the actual idea underneath. Try it once on any topic and you will reach for it again.

---

## Step 4: Ask AI what it is not sure about

This is the step most people skip. AI sounds confident even when it should not. Ask it to flag the gaps.

> "What parts of this topic are still uncertain or debated? What should I be careful about when reading more on this?"

This is how you use AI responsibly, not just quickly.

---

## Step 5: Ask for a different angle

Before you close the conversation, ask AI to challenge what it just told you.

> "Is there a different way to look at this? What would someone who disagrees with the mainstream view say?"

You do not have to agree with the counterargument. The point is to know it exists.

---

## Step 6: Ask for a TL;DR

Here is something most people do not realize: AI remembers everything in your session. Every question, every answer, every follow-up. You can ask it to compress the whole conversation into something you can save and use later.

Try this:

> "TL;DR: summarize everything we just covered in one short paragraph I can keep or share with someone."

**TL;DR** stands for "Too Long; Didn't Read." It tells AI to skip to the point. You can use it in any session, on any topic, whenever a conversation has gone long and you want the short version.

Save what it gives you. The next time this topic comes up at work, you have a clean, usable brief ready.

---

## You just learned three real things

Chained prompting turns a single question into a proper research session. Each prompt you write is informed by the answer before it, which is what makes the output useful.

ELI10 and TL;DR are reusable techniques you can carry into any conversation, with any AI tool, on any topic. They are not tricks. They are shortcuts that experienced users reach for all the time.

AI remembers your whole session. A good research conversation should always end with a summary you can save.
