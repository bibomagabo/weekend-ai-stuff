The last two weeks, you introduced yourself to AI and had it look at your real workflow. You've been having conversations with AI. This week, you learn how to brief it properly.

Most people type whatever comes to mind and hope AI figures it out. Sometimes it works. Usually it doesn't. Prompt engineering is the skill of giving AI a clear brief so it delivers what you actually need on the first try. And it's simpler than it sounds.

You're going to learn a framework called RICO. Four elements, one line each, and you'll see the difference immediately.

---

## Step 1: Open Gemini and paste your Digital Persona

If you built your Digital Persona in Week 1, paste it into a new Gemini chat first. This gives AI your context before you even start.

If you haven't done that challenge yet, no worries. You can skip this step and fill in a bit more detail in the prompt below.

---

## Step 2: Understand the RICO framework

RICO stands for Role, Instructions, Context, and Output. Each one tells AI something specific about what you need.

**[Role]** — Who do you want AI to be? A tutor, a coach, a writing assistant, an analyst. This shapes how it responds.

**[Instructions]** — What do you want it to do? Be specific. "Help me learn" is vague. "Teach me the basics, one concept at a time" is clear.

**[Context]** — What should AI know about your situation? Your experience level, your constraints, what you're working with. The more relevant detail here, the better the output.

**[Output]** — What should the final result look like? A plan, a summary, a list of steps, a draft. Tell AI what format you want.

That's it. Four elements. One line each is enough to start.

---

## Step 3: Try this sample prompt

Here's a prompt built using RICO. Copy it, fill in the variables in curly brackets, and paste it into Gemini.

> **[Role]**
> You are a patient and practical tutor who explains things in simple terms with real-world examples.
>
> **[Instructions]**
> Teach me the basics of {the skill or topic you want to learn, e.g., basic data analysis, project management, personal finance, public speaking}. Start with what I need to know first, then build from there. Check if I understood each part before moving on.
>
> **[Context]**
> I'm a complete beginner in this topic. I learn best when I can relate new ideas to things I already know. When explaining concepts, use examples related to {a topic or interest you enjoy, e.g., K-Pop, basketball, cooking, anime, travel}. I have about 30 minutes for this session.
>
> **[Output]**
> Break the lesson into 3 to 5 short sections I can go through one at a time. At the end, give me one small exercise to test what I learned.
>
> Before you start, ask me 2 to 3 questions so you know exactly where to begin.

If you saved your Workflow Discovery roadmap from last week, check it for skills or topics you wanted to explore. That's a good place to pick your {skill or topic} from.

---

## Step 4: Watch what happens at the end

Notice the last line: *"Before you start, ask me 2 to 3 questions so you know exactly where to begin."*

This is called calibration. Instead of letting AI guess what you need, you're telling it to check with you first. Most beginners skip this and accept whatever AI gives them on the first try. You're not doing that anymore.

When AI asks you those clarifying questions, answer them honestly. The lesson it builds after that will be noticeably better than if you let it run without asking.

---

## Step 5: Build your own RICO prompt

Now try it yourself. Pick a different task, anything you're working on or curious about, and write your own prompt using the four elements.

It doesn't need to be long. One line per element is a solid start. Add the calibration line at the end. Paste it into Gemini and see what you get.

If you're stuck, start with one of these:

- Ask AI to help you prepare for a meeting or presentation
- Ask AI to explain a concept you keep hearing about but don't fully understand
- Ask AI to help you organize your week

---

## You just learned three real things:

- That a structured prompt gets you better results than a random question
- That RICO (Role, Instructions, Context, Output) gives you a reusable format for any AI conversation
- That asking AI to calibrate with you first puts you in control of the output, not the other way around
